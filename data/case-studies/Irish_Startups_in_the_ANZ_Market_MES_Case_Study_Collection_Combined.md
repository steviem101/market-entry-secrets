# Irish Startups in the ANZ Market – MES Case Study Collection

## About This Collection

Irish technology companies supported by Enterprise Ireland exported more than €483 million to Australia and New Zealand in the latest reporting year, with export growth to Australia increasing by 58% over the past five years [cite:127]. The five companies profiled here — Daon, Fenergo, FINEOS, Wayflyer, and Tines — represent some of the most instructive ANZ market entry stories in that cohort. Together, they span identity and biometrics, financial compliance, insurance software, e-commerce finance, and AI workflow automation. Each took a distinct path into the market, but all share common traits: early commitment to building in-market presence, alignment with local regulatory and commercial urgency, and disciplined land-and-expand execution [cite:127].

## Case Study 1: Daon
### Winning Government Trust with Biometrics — From Border Crossings to Banking

**Sector:** Digital Identity & Biometrics  
**Founded:** 2000, Dublin, Ireland  
**Founder:** Dermot Desmond  
**ANZ Entry:** Mid-2000s (New Zealand); formalised with Canberra office  
**Stage at ANZ Entry:** Established (post-US government growth)

#### The Company

Daon is a biometrics and identity assurance software company founded in Dublin in 2000 by Irish entrepreneur Dermot Desmond [cite:98]. The company builds software that verifies and authenticates people across the identity lifecycle using facial recognition, voice recognition, fingerprints, and other biometric factors [cite:104]. Its platform, IdentityX, serves clients across banking, telecoms, government, and critical infrastructure in over 80 countries [cite:17][cite:107].

#### Why ANZ?

The ANZ region offered Daon a strong fit because New Zealand government agencies were early adopters of national digital identity infrastructure, while Australia had rising demand for secure digital onboarding and fraud prevention in financial services [cite:75][cite:57]. These are trust-sensitive sectors where biometric identity assurance creates outsized value [cite:57].

#### The Market Entry Journey

Daon's ANZ expansion began through New Zealand government use cases. New Zealand passports used Daon's facial recognition technology, and in 2018 the Department of Internal Affairs integrated Daon's IdentityX into the RealMe Now mobile app so citizens could create a verified digital identity remotely using facial recognition [cite:62][cite:80]. In 2023, New Zealand's Ministry of Social Development selected Daon to provide face biometrics for identity verification for financial support programs, extending Daon's government credibility into another high-trust public service [cite:59].

Daon later reinforced its regional commitment with a dedicated office in Canberra, chosen to serve government and critical infrastructure customers in Australia and New Zealand [cite:22][cite:101]. It also invested in senior local leadership through roles such as SVP, ANZ & Southeast Asia and Regional Director, Australia & New Zealand, which gave customers access to long-tenured executives rather than only remote sales support [cite:20][cite:28].

In Australia, Daon's positioning broadened into banking, telecoms, and customer authentication. Its biometric onboarding, authentication, and deepfake detection capabilities address fraud, contact-centre risk, and digital identity verification challenges relevant to ANZ institutions [cite:2][cite:57].

#### Key Outcomes

- New Zealand government partner for passport facial recognition, RealMe identity infrastructure, and MSD benefit access [cite:59][cite:62][cite:80]
- Canberra presence serving Australian and New Zealand government and critical infrastructure needs [cite:22][cite:101]
- Dedicated ANZ regional leadership at senior executive level [cite:20][cite:28]
- Expanded relevance in ANZ banking, telco, and fraud prevention workflows [cite:2][cite:57]

#### What You Can Copy

| Playbook move | Why it worked |
|---|---|
| Use government as the anchor customer | New Zealand public-sector wins created trust that private-sector buyers could recognise [cite:59][cite:62] |
| Base near the buyer | Canberra positioned Daon close to government and critical infrastructure buyers [cite:22][cite:101] |
| Invest in executive-grade local leadership | Trust-heavy markets reward senior local decision-makers and continuity [cite:20][cite:28] |
| Expand from one proof point into adjacent sectors | Government credibility helped Daon address banking and telecom identity needs [cite:2][cite:57] |

## Case Study 2: Fenergo
### Regulatory Urgency as a Wedge — How Fenergo Won Four of Australia's Biggest Banks

**Sector:** RegTech / Client Lifecycle Management  
**Founded:** 2009, Dublin, Ireland  
**Founders:** Marc Murphy, Philip Burke, Cian Kinsella  
**ANZ Entry:** September 2014 (Sydney)  
**Stage at ANZ Entry:** Growth-stage

#### The Company

Fenergo was founded in Dublin in 2009 to help financial institutions digitise client lifecycle management, including onboarding, KYC, AML, and ongoing regulatory compliance [cite:40][cite:94]. Its growth has been driven by increasing compliance complexity across global financial markets, and by FY2025 it reported €149.4 million in revenue and €21.1 million in profit before tax [cite:36].

#### Why ANZ?

Australia offered concentrated demand because its major banks faced escalating AML, KYC, and regulatory scrutiny, especially in the broader context of AUSTRAC enforcement and the Royal Commission era [cite:113]. A small number of top-tier banking wins in Australia could create outsized regional credibility because the market is dominated by a few large institutions [cite:33].

#### The Market Entry Journey

Fenergo formally entered APAC in September 2014 by setting up a local team in Sydney during an Enterprise Ireland trade mission to Australia [cite:6]. The company hired Brett Hodge as Head of Sales for APAC, bringing years of regional financial services experience into the business from the start [cite:6].

Its pitch to Australian banks centred on solving urgent compliance pain: automating onboarding, reducing KYC and AML friction, and helping institutions cope with growing regulatory change [cite:24]. By October 2017, Fenergo had been selected by four of Australia's top five banks, including institutions such as Westpac, Macquarie, ANZ Bank, Commonwealth Bank, and NAB in its APAC customer story [cite:30][cite:31].

Fenergo also used a “community approach” in Australia, bringing banks together to shape regulatory rule interpretations and product evolution, which increased buy-in and created higher switching costs [cite:24]. Sydney then became the base for broader APAC expansion, with Singapore opening in 2016 and additional expansion into Japan following later [cite:37][cite:27].

#### Key Outcomes

- Formal APAC entry via Sydney in 2014 [cite:6]
- Four of Australia's top five banks selected Fenergo by 2017 [cite:30]
- Sydney used as the foundation for wider APAC expansion [cite:37][cite:27]
- Strong financial performance and ongoing global expansion [cite:36]

#### What You Can Copy

| Playbook move | Why it worked |
|---|---|
| Enter when pain is urgent, not abstract | Regulatory pressure made the purchase non-optional for banks [cite:24][cite:113] |
| Use trade-mission momentum | Enterprise Ireland support improved market access and credibility [cite:6] |
| Hire a senior local first employee | Deep local market knowledge accelerated enterprise sales [cite:6] |
| Turn customers into a compliance community | Shared rule-building created loyalty and product depth [cite:24] |
| Use Sydney as an APAC launchpad | A strong Australian base enabled further regional expansion [cite:37][cite:27] |

## Case Study 3: FINEOS
### Category Dominance Through Vertical Focus — How FINEOS Became the Standard for ANZ Insurance

**Sector:** Insurance Software  
**Founded:** 1993, Dublin, Ireland  
**Founder:** Michael Kelly  
**ANZ Entry:** Early 2000s; landmark NZ deal in 2005  
**Stage at ANZ Entry:** Bootstrapped growth

#### The Company

FINEOS was founded in Dublin in 1993 by Michael Kelly to build specialist software for life, accident, and health insurers [cite:47]. The company focused on replacing legacy insurance systems with a modern platform spanning claims, policy, billing, and administration [cite:43][cite:45].

#### Why ANZ?

ANZ offered a structurally attractive insurance market. New Zealand's national accident compensation framework created a unique government-scale claims environment, while Australia's life insurance sector — including insurance linked to superannuation — created a large and specialised market for claims and policy administration technology [cite:111][cite:52].

#### The Market Entry Journey

The defining early ANZ win came in 2005 when New Zealand's Accident Compensation Corporation selected FINEOS to replace its legacy claims management platform in a contract valued at NZ$39 million [cite:116]. The system later supported over 2 million claims annually across 48 locations, making it one of New Zealand's largest public sector IT programs at the time [cite:111][cite:119].

That ACC credibility opened the door to other government and quasi-government clients in the region, including the NZ Defence Force, Victoria's TAC, WorkSafe Victoria, and NSW's icare group [cite:7]. FINEOS also won major Australian private-sector insurance share, with FINEOS Claims adopted by carriers representing over 70% of Australian life premiums and 13 ANZ insurance clients live in production [cite:7].

The company deepened execution capacity through a strategic ANZ partnership with Sequential in 2017, combining FINEOS software with local consulting, change management, and optimisation support [cite:12]. In 2019, FINEOS listed on the ASX, raising A$211 million in the largest IPO on the exchange that year, underscoring how central Australia and New Zealand had become to its growth story [cite:96][cite:108].

#### Key Outcomes

- Landmark 2005 ACC New Zealand contract [cite:116]
- Strong presence across ANZ public-sector compensation and life insurance [cite:7][cite:111]
- More than 70% of Australian life premiums represented on FINEOS Claims [cite:7]
- 13 ANZ insurance clients live in production [cite:7]
- ASX listing in 2019 raised A$211 million [cite:96][cite:108]

#### What You Can Copy

| Playbook move | Why it worked |
|---|---|
| Go deep into one regulated vertical | FINEOS focused on insurance and built category authority [cite:43][cite:45] |
| Land a government-scale proof point | ACC validated the platform at national scale [cite:116][cite:119] |
| Use public-sector credibility to win private-sector accounts | Government trust helped unlock insurer adoption [cite:7][cite:111] |
| Support software with local consulting partners | Sequential improved delivery confidence and outcomes [cite:12] |
| Treat ANZ as a strategic capital market too | The ASX listing reinforced long-term commitment and visibility [cite:96][cite:108] |

## Case Study 4: Wayflyer
### Funding Growth at the Speed of E-Commerce — Wayflyer's Australian Playbook

**Sector:** Revenue-Based Financing / E-Commerce Growth Capital  
**Founded:** 2019, Dublin, Ireland  
**Founders:** Aidan Corbett and Jack Pierse  
**ANZ Entry:** 2021 (Sydney)  
**Stage at ANZ Entry:** Early growth

#### The Company

Wayflyer was founded in Dublin in 2019 to provide non-dilutive growth capital to e-commerce brands, using sales performance data to underwrite advances for inventory and marketing [cite:126][cite:81]. The company scaled quickly, reaching unicorn status in 2022 and later reporting €95.2 million in FY2024 revenue [cite:129].

#### Why ANZ?

Australia was identified early as a high-priority expansion market because e-commerce brands there faced the same working-capital gaps as sellers in larger markets, but had fewer tailored funding options [cite:81][cite:95]. The local market was digitally mature and full of growing direct-to-consumer brands that needed fast access to inventory and marketing capital [cite:95].

#### The Market Entry Journey

Wayflyer launched in Australia in 2021 and established a Sydney office at 100 Market Street, giving it a true local operating base rather than just offshore support [cite:103]. By early 2022, it had already deployed AUD$60 million to more than 150 Australian e-commerce brands [cite:95]. Named Australian customers and examples from Wayflyer-linked storytelling include AMR Hair & Beauty, Bhumi Organic Cotton, Petz Park, Stax, King Kong Apparel, Black Swallow, and Bohemian Traders [cite:95][cite:97][cite:83].

Wayflyer's advantage was not only product design but capital access. Its large debt facilities, including funding from J.P. Morgan and later ATLAS SP Partners, helped it compete more effectively on speed and scale in markets like Australia [cite:69][cite:13]. Its infrastructure partnerships also helped it deploy capital efficiently across markets [cite:86].

By 2025, Wayflyer had deployed $5 billion to more than 5,000 brands across 11 countries, showing that its operating model could scale globally while continuing to support ANZ growth [cite:76].

#### Key Outcomes

- Sydney office established in 2021 [cite:103]
- AUD$60 million deployed to 150+ Australian brands by early 2022 [cite:95]
- Strong set of Australian customer examples and success stories [cite:95][cite:97][cite:83]
- Global scale of $5 billion deployed across 5,000+ brands in 11 countries [cite:76]
- Institutional capital support from major global lenders [cite:69][cite:13]

#### What You Can Copy

| Playbook move | Why it worked |
|---|---|
| Enter ANZ early in the company journey | Wayflyer treated Australia as a core growth market, not a late add-on [cite:81][cite:95] |
| Put a real team on the ground | A Sydney office improved trust, responsiveness, and customer acquisition [cite:103] |
| Lead with outcome stories from local brands | Australian merchant stories made the model tangible [cite:95][cite:97] |
| Bring global capital into local SME gaps | Large facilities helped Wayflyer compete where banks were underserving the market [cite:69][cite:13] |
| Position ANZ merchants as part of a global commerce network | Cross-border relevance strengthened the value proposition [cite:76] |

## Case Study 5: Tines
### From Security Automation to AI Workflows — Tines' APAC Beachhead Strategy

**Sector:** Workflow Automation / Security Automation  
**Founded:** 2018, Dublin, Ireland  
**Founders:** Eoin Hinchy and Thomas Kinsella  
**ANZ Entry:** Customer-led traction before formal hub expansion; APAC hub formalised in 2026  
**Stage at ANZ Entry:** Early growth

#### The Company

Tines was founded in Dublin in 2018 by Eoin Hinchy and Thomas Kinsella to eliminate manual, repetitive work in security and operations through no-code workflow automation [cite:70]. The company later reached unicorn status in 2025 after a $125 million Series C round that valued it at $1.125 billion [cite:109][cite:112].

#### Why ANZ?

ANZ became attractive because enterprise teams in Australia and New Zealand faced the same automation and security pressures as US firms, but often operated with leaner teams. Early customer traction with marquee Australian names gave Tines a credible base for wider growth [cite:23][cite:125].

#### The Market Entry Journey

Canva became one of Tines' most important reference customers in ANZ, helping the company prove that its no-code automation platform could scale security detections and responses inside a globally recognised Australian technology company [cite:128][cite:137]. That kind of flagship validation mattered because it created peer credibility before Tines had fully formalised its local footprint [cite:125].

As local demand grew, Tines built out dedicated ANZ capacity. In 2026 it announced plans to double Australian headcount and launch an APAC hub in Australia, naming customers such as Canva, REA Group, Australian National University, and nib Health Funds as evidence of momentum [cite:23]. Tines also leaned on local ecosystem relationships, including a partnership with Restack Technology, and supported enterprise buying requirements such as AWS-based local data residency [cite:82][cite:61].

The company now positions itself beyond only security use cases. It reports that 75% of active customers use the platform across multiple teams and that 94% of pilots move into production, showing a strong land-and-expand motion that is relevant to ANZ organisations pursuing practical automation and AI deployment [cite:23].

#### Key Outcomes

- Marquee ANZ customers including Canva, REA Group, ANU, and nib Health Funds [cite:23]
- Formal APAC hub expansion in Australia announced in 2026 [cite:23]
- Australian headcount growth and local data residency support [cite:23][cite:61]
- High pilot-to-production conversion and broad cross-team expansion model [cite:23]

#### What You Can Copy

| Playbook move | Why it worked |
|---|---|
| Let a marquee customer validate the category | Canva gave Tines immediate local credibility [cite:128][cite:137] |
| Formalise the hub after demand is proven | Tines turned customer-led traction into an official APAC footprint [cite:23] |
| Solve procurement blockers early | Local data residency matters in ANZ enterprise buying [cite:61] |
| Use local partners for delivery depth | Restack strengthened local ecosystem fit [cite:82] |
| Expand from one team into many | The platform's land-and-expand motion suits lean ANZ organisations [cite:23] |

## Cross-Case Patterns

These five companies entered ANZ in different sectors, but their success patterns were strikingly similar. They committed to local presence, found a high-trust anchor customer, aligned with a pressing market problem, and then expanded outward from that first proof point [cite:6][cite:7][cite:22][cite:23][cite:95].

| Pattern | Daon | Fenergo | FINEOS | Wayflyer | Tines |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dedicated local presence | Canberra office [cite:22][cite:101] | Sydney APAC base [cite:6][cite:27] | 130+ ANZ employees by IPO [cite:48] | Sydney office [cite:103] | Australia APAC hub [cite:23] |
| Anchor client | NZ government [cite:59][cite:62] | Four major banks [cite:30] | ACC NZ [cite:116] | Australian e-commerce brands [cite:95][cite:97] | Canva [cite:128][cite:137] |
| Expansion method | Government to adjacent sectors [cite:57] | Regulatory wedge into Tier-1 banks [cite:24] | Vertical dominance in insurance [cite:7] | Capital deployment into underserved brands [cite:69][cite:95] | Customer-led proof then hub buildout [cite:23] |
