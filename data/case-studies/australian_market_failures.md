### Case 1 — Starbucks

**Overview**

Starbucks is one of the most-cited examples of a global brand misjudging Australian consumers. The US coffee giant entered Australia in 2000 with the same playbook that had worked in markets without an established café culture — only to find Australia had one of the world's most sophisticated and fiercely local espresso traditions.

**Australia Entry Strategy**

Starbucks launched in Sydney in 2000 and rapidly expanded to 84 stores across New South Wales, Victoria, Queensland, ACT, South Australia and Tasmania. The strategy was a direct copy-paste of the US model: large-format stores, sweetened signature drinks, and rapid suburban expansion. No adjustment was made for local tastes, existing price norms, or the deeply personal relationship Australians had with their local barista.

**What Went Wrong in Australia**

In its first seven years, Starbucks accumulated A$105 million in losses. The company took on A$54 million in loans from the US parent just to stay solvent by 2007. The core problems were:
- **Misread culture**: Australians already had a rich espresso culture characterised by personalised service, strong flat whites, and affordable pricing at independent cafés. Starbucks' sweeter, more commercialised drinks felt alien.
- **Overexpansion without earned trust**: Starbucks opened stores before Australian consumers had chosen to adopt the brand, artificially forcing coverage rather than growing organically from urban hubs where the tourist and student population was higher.
- **Pricing**: Starbucks was significantly more expensive than local alternatives, without offering a quality differential that Australians could perceive.

**Outcome in Australia**

On 29 July 2008, Starbucks announced the closure of 61 of its 84 stores, with 640 jobs lost. In 2014, the company handed over the remaining 24 stores to the Withers Group, which operates 7-Eleven in Australia. Today, ~61 stores remain — primarily in CBD tourist zones and international airports — serving largely foreign visitors rather than locals.

**Lessons for MES**
- **Do not assume brand equity travels**: Global recognition does not substitute for earned local trust.
- **Established local cultures are moats**: Markets with deep existing preferences (coffee, food, sport) require category re-definition, not replication.
- **Start urban and organic, not suburban and forced**: Rapid coverage expansion before consumer adoption locks in fixed costs against weak revenue.
- **Adapt the product to the market**: Starbucks' core beverage range was too sweet and too generic for Australian coffee drinkers.
- **Study the competitive set honestly**: The threat was not other chains but 60,000+ independent cafés with established customer loyalty.

### Case 2 — Lowe's / Masters Home Improvement

**Overview**

The joint venture between US hardware giant Lowe's and Australian retail conglomerate Woolworths is widely regarded as the most catastrophic retail failure in Australian business history, accumulating more than A$3.2 billion in losses over seven years.

**Australia Entry Strategy**

In August 2009, Woolworths announced a joint venture with Lowe's to enter the home improvement market, targeting Bunnings Warehouse (owned by rival Wesfarmers). The plan called for 150 stores within five years. The venture was internally dubbed "Project Oxygen" — designed to "suck the oxygen" out of Bunnings. The first Masters store opened in September 2011.

**What Went Wrong in Australia**

- **Wrong partner dynamics**: Lowe's brought its US Northern Hemisphere seasonal product schedules to an Australian market with opposite seasons, creating systematic misalignment in garden and hardware ranging.
- **Location errors**: Bunnings had already secured the best large-format hardware sites. Masters was forced into secondary locations.
- **Supplier coercion**: Bunnings reportedly threatened to delist suppliers who also stocked Masters, starving the new entrant of key brands.
- **No clear value proposition**: Masters charged higher prices than Bunnings and offered an inferior product range, failing to give Australian DIY shoppers a compelling reason to switch.
- **Strategic distraction**: The hardware venture diverted Woolworths' attention from its core grocery business, which lost ground to Coles during the same period.
- **JV governance failure**: The two partners had fundamentally different views on strategy, leading to costly court battles during the wind-down.

**Outcome in Australia**

In January 2016, Lowe's announced the venture was unprofitable and requested an exit. All 63 Masters stores were closed by 11 December 2016. Woolworths exited hardware entirely and Bunnings was left with near-monopoly control of the market.

**Lessons for MES**
- **JV structures require aligned incentives**: Mismatched strategic goals between JV partners are fatal, especially in asset-heavy retail.
- **Incumbent entrenchment is a real barrier**: Bunnings' supplier relationships and site control created structural moats that capital alone cannot overcome.
- **Seasonal and operational localisation is non-negotiable**: Product ranging, seasonal campaigns, and even shelf layouts must reflect local conditions.
- **Test before scaling**: An 80-store rollout without a validated pilot in Australian conditions was an enormous risk.
- **Distraction costs are real**: Entering a new vertical while the core business is under competitive pressure compounds risk catastrophically.

### Case 3 — Deliveroo

**Overview**

Deliveroo was one of the first app-based food delivery platforms in Australia, launching in Melbourne in 2015. After seven years, it exited the market in November 2022 after being unable to achieve a top-two market position against Uber Eats and DoorDash.

**Australia Entry Strategy**

Deliveroo launched in Melbourne in 2015 with a premium positioning — partnering only with "high-quality" restaurants and promising fast, reliable delivery. This niche strategy was intended to differentiate it from mass-market competitors. At its peak, the platform was used by approximately 12,000 hospitality venues.

**What Went Wrong in Australia**

- **Market position erosion**: Deliveroo held a leading position in 2016–17 but was rapidly overtaken as Uber Eats and DoorDash expanded their restaurant networks and invested heavily in consumer subsidies.
- **Premium niche vs. scale economics**: Food delivery is a scale game. Limiting restaurant partnerships to high-quality venues reduced order volume, while competitors with broader networks captured more of the market.
- **Unit economics**: In the first half of 2022, Australia represented approximately 3% of Deliveroo's total gross transaction value — far below viable scale.
- **Venture capital withdrawal**: As global VC funding contracted in 2022, the company could no longer justify "a disproportionate level of investment which would have highly uncertain returns" in Australia.
- **Regulatory pressure on gig workers**: Australia's growing regulatory scrutiny of contractor classification added compliance cost to the labour model.

**Outcome in Australia**

On 15 November 2022, Deliveroo placed its Australian subsidiary into voluntary administration and immediately ceased all orders. Administrators KordaMentha wound down the business. Approximately 15,000 restaurant partners and riders lost their income with virtually no notice.

**Lessons for MES**
- **Marketplaces require top-two position or exit**: Deliveroo's own global strategy was explicit: only operate where you can be #1 or #2. Australia proved this rule correct.
- **Niche premium positioning in commodity markets is fragile**: When competitors offer lower prices and wider choice, quality differentiation alone is insufficient.
- **Gig economy models carry regulatory risk in Australia**: Australia's Fair Work framework and progressive regulation of contractor status must be modelled as a real cost.
- **Fund to lead, not to survive**: Entering a capital-intensive marketplace without the funding to outlast incumbents is a losing bet.
- **Exit planning matters**: Deliveroo's abrupt exit left thousands of riders and venues with unpaid obligations — a reputational cautionary tale.

### Case 4 — Menulog / Just Eat Takeaway

**Overview**

Menulog was founded in Australia in 2006 as one of the country's first online food ordering platforms. After a 2015 acquisition by UK-based Just Eat (later Just Eat Takeaway.com), it became a foreign-owned operation and ultimately failed to maintain relevance. It ceased operations on 26 November 2025 after nearly 20 years.

**Australia Entry Strategy**

Menulog was originally a domestic startup and was therefore an acquisition-led "entry" by Just Eat in 2015. Just Eat Takeaway.com retained the Menulog brand and invested heavily in marketing, most famously signing global celebrity ambassadors including Snoop Dogg, Katy Perry, and Christina Aguilera.

**What Went Wrong in Australia**

- **High-profile marketing did not translate to market share**: Despite extraordinary celebrity campaigns, Menulog's share remained 5–10% while Uber Eats commanded 85–90% of the food delivery market.
- **Failed to invest in logistics infrastructure**: Unlike Uber Eats and DoorDash, Menulog was slow to build its own delivery fleet, relying on restaurants to self-deliver for much of its history — a structural disadvantage as consumers valued speed and reliability.
- **Deliveroo's collapse gave DoorDash the opportunity**: After Deliveroo exited in 2022, DoorDash absorbed most of its market share, not Menulog — indicating brand weakness in Australian consumer minds.
- **Consecutive years of significant operating losses**: The business was structurally unprofitable in a market dominated by a competitor with massive cross-sell advantages (Uber's rideshare user base feeding Uber Eats).
- **International strategic misalignment**: Parent company Just Eat Takeaway was under significant financial pressure globally and ultimately chose to "focus on accelerating growth in other markets."

**Outcome in Australia**

Menulog ceased operations on 26 November 2025, with approximately 120 staff made redundant and tens of thousands of restaurant partners and couriers losing the platform as a revenue channel. The closure left Uber Eats and DoorDash as the only two major players, raising duopoly concerns.

**Lessons for MES**
- **Marketing spend without product parity is wasted capital**: Celebrity campaigns cannot substitute for structural platform superiority.
- **Acquisition of a local player does not guarantee market leadership**: Just Eat bought market entry, not market leadership.
- **Delivery logistics infrastructure is a durable competitive moat**: In food delivery, owning the rider network beats marketplace-only models.
- **Understand parent company global priorities**: When a subsidiary's home country conflicts with the parent's global investment thesis, the subsidiary is vulnerable.
- **Two-player markets emerge quickly in on-demand delivery**: Enter with a funded plan to be #1 or #2, or don't enter at all.

### Case 5 — Ola

**Overview**

Ola, India's dominant rideshare company, entered Australia in 2018 as one of three Uber challengers. After six years of losses and a strategic pivot ahead of an Indian stock market listing, Ola abruptly ceased Australian operations in April 2024 with just two days' notice.

**Australia Entry Strategy**

Ola launched in Perth in 2018 and expanded to Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane, the Gold Coast, Adelaide, and Canberra. It used a price-competition strategy common to rideshare challengers globally — subsidised fares to attract drivers and riders — hoping to build enough critical mass to survive as a viable #2 player behind Uber.

**What Went Wrong in Australia**

- **Never achieved profitability**: Ola reported no profitable quarter in Australia across its six years of operation.
- **Uber's dominance was unassailable**: Uber had a multi-year head start and strong brand recognition. Ola's promotional pricing attracted deal-seekers rather than loyal users.
- **Scaled back in 2020**: The pandemic caused Ola to dramatically reduce Australian operations in 2020, and the company had not posted on social media since 2021 — a visible signal of declining investment.
- **Indian priorities reasserted**: Ola was preparing for an Indian IPO (and had already listed its electric vehicle subsidiary, Ola Electric). International markets became a distraction from the domestic growth story needed for the listing.
- **Abrupt exit created reputational damage**: The two-day notice left 1.5 million users and hundreds of drivers with no transition support, drawing criticism from the Transport Workers Union.

**Outcome in Australia**

Ola's Australian and New Zealand operations closed on 12 April 2024. The company simultaneously exited the UK, consolidating operations entirely in India. Its exit left the Australian rideshare market as a Uber-dominated duopoly with only DiDi as the remaining challenger.

**Lessons for MES**
- **Subsidised-growth market entry requires commitment to profitability phases**: Entering with price subsidies without a clear path to unit economics is burning money on borrowed time.
- **Two-day exit notices are a brand-destroying legacy**: How you exit a market is remembered and affects future re-entry possibilities.
- **Be honest about parent company capital allocation priorities**: If the home market demands all capital (as an IPO story), international subsidiaries face existential risk.
- **Rideshare and marketplace businesses require critical mass**: Sub-scale position in a network effects business is a structural loss-making position.
- **Regulatory risk compounds operational risk**: Australia's evolving gig economy regulations added cost to a business already losing money.

### Case 6 — Affirm

**Overview**

US-based buy now, pay later (BNPL) platform Affirm entered Australia in late 2021 via a single merchant partnership with Peloton. Less than 18 months later, it exited — the first country Affirm had ever left.

**Australia Entry Strategy**

Affirm's Australian entry was piggyback: it partnered exclusively with Peloton Australia to finance the purchase of exercise bikes and treadmills via instalment payments. This was a "single merchant" model, meaning Affirm had no independent Australian merchant base or consumer brand.

**What Went Wrong in Australia**

- **Single-merchant dependency**: Affirm's entire Australian business was built on Peloton — when Peloton struggled globally (post-COVID fitness decline, treadmill recalls), Affirm's Australian revenue collapsed simultaneously.
- **Saturated, mature BNPL market**: Australia already had the world's most developed BNPL ecosystem, with Afterpay (Block-owned), Zip, Klarna, and PayPal all established before Affirm arrived.
- **No path to merchant diversification**: Affirm noted in its annual filings that Australian operations remained "on a more limited basis" — a red flag that was never addressed before exit.
- **Global macro pressure**: In early 2023, Affirm laid off 19% of its global workforce. Australia — representing negligible revenue — was an easy cut.

**Outcome in Australia**

Affirm began winding down Australian operations from 28 February 2023 and exited fully shortly thereafter. It was the first country Affirm had ever closed. Peloton Australia moved its financing to rival Zip. Affirm redirected investment to North American and UK markets.

**Lessons for MES**
- **Never enter a market on the back of a single commercial partner**: One merchant = one point of failure.
- **Assess market maturity before entry**: Australia's BNPL market was already crowded with well-funded local incumbents. Late entry with less capital is a structural disadvantage.
- **BNPL requires regulatory runway**: Australia's evolving BNPL regulation (consumer credit obligations, ASIC scrutiny) adds compliance cost that smaller international players cannot absorb.
- **Align international entry with global strategic trajectory**: If profitability focus is in core markets, international ventures become the first casualties.
- **Size the market opportunity honestly**: Australia's 26 million population means even #1 BNPL market share is modest for a company with US-sized cost structures.

### Case 7 — Laybuy

**Overview**

Laybuy was a New Zealand-founded BNPL company that ASX-listed in September 2020 to fund UK and Australian expansion. After a brutal combination of post-COVID retail decline, rising defaults, cyberattacks, and a failed sale process, it entered receivership in June 2024.

**Australia Entry Strategy**

Founded in Auckland in 2017, Laybuy expanded into Australia and the UK, building a base of approximately 766,000 customers and 10,500 merchants globally. It listed on the ASX in 2020 specifically to access capital for this international expansion, raising funds from retail investors who were enthusiastic about the BNPL sector's pandemic-era boom.

**What Went Wrong in Australia**

- **Listed at the peak of BNPL mania**: The ASX IPO occurred at the exact top of the BNPL valuation cycle. Rising interest rates in 2022–23 fundamentally broke the model — Laybuy funded interest-free credit via loans, and rising borrowing costs destroyed unit economics.
- **Credit losses accelerated**: Consumer spending downturn increased defaults and fraud, particularly in UK operations, further draining capital.
- **Failed sale process**: Between December 2023 and April 2024, directors sought a buyer or new investment. A deal fell through at the last minute.
- **Delisted in 2023**: Laybuy was delisted from the ASX in March 2023 — before its collapse — eliminating its ability to raise equity capital.
- **Market concentration**: Afterpay and Zip dominated Australian BNPL, leaving no space for a smaller, less well-capitalised regional player.

**Outcome in Australia**

Laybuy Group Holdings, Laybuy Holdings, and Laybuy Australia Pty Ltd were all placed into receivership on 17 June 2024 by Deloitte. Klarna acquired Laybuy's customer base and technology platform from the receivers.

**Lessons for MES**
- **Timing market entry around funding cycles matters**: IPOing a capital-hungry fintech at the top of a sector bubble creates fragility when conditions change.
- **Interest rate sensitivity must be modelled for BNPL**: A model that funds interest-free credit through debt is existentially vulnerable to rate hikes.
- **Niche BNPL players need a defensible vertical or geography**: Without a protected niche, smaller players are outspent and outcompeted by global and local giants.
- **Cross-border operations amplify risk**: Simultaneous Australia, NZ, and UK expansion tripled complexity and cost without tripling revenue.
- **Have a secondary capital source**: Sole reliance on public markets for a loss-making startup is extremely fragile.

### Case 8 — Topshop

**Overview**

British fashion giant Topshop entered Australia in 2011 via a local franchise/licence arrangement with Austradia Pty Ltd. The franchise collapsed into voluntary administration in May 2017 — and the sole surviving Sydney store eventually closed in 2020 when COVID hit.

**Australia Entry Strategy**

Unlike Uniqlo and H&M — which entered Australia as fully owned subsidiaries — Topshop operated through an independent Australian franchisee, Austradia, which paid a licence fee to Topshop UK and purchased stock from the UK parent. At its peak, Topshop/Topman operated nine standalone stores, 17 concessions within Myer, and an online business.

**What Went Wrong in Australia**

- **Franchise fee structure inflated costs**: The licence fees paid to Topshop UK, combined with the cost of importing goods from the UK, made Austradia's effective cost structure significantly higher than vertically integrated competitors like H&M and Uniqlo.
- **Vertical competitors undercut**: H&M and Zara owned their supply chains end-to-end, enabling lower prices. Topshop could not match them while paying franchise fees.
- **Currency risk**: The weak Australian dollar against the pound in 2015–17 made UK-sourced goods materially more expensive.
- **High mall rents**: Austradia had taken on premium mall locations with high per-square-metre rent commitments, requiring unrealistic sales targets to break even.
- **Myer partnership added complexity**: The concession model within Myer (in which Myer held a 20% interest in Austradia) added a layer of governance complexity without solving the underlying cost problems.

**Outcome in Australia**

Austradia entered voluntary administration in May 2017, with administrator Ferrier Hodgson managing the process. Topshop UK worked with the administrator to attempt restructuring. A single Sydney store remained open into 2020 before finally closing during COVID. Topshop UK itself later collapsed globally in 2020 due to Arcadia Group's bankruptcy.

**Lessons for MES**
- **Franchise/licence structures are cost disadvantages vs. owned operations**: A middleman layer between brand owner and consumer always inflates the cost stack.
- **Study vertically integrated competitors before entering**: If your direct competitors own their supply chains and you don't, your pricing will always be disadvantaged.
- **Mall rent is a fixed commitment regardless of performance**: High base rents require high sustained revenue — a bet that rarely pays off in fashion.
- **JV with local retailer does not solve structural problems**: Myer's stake in Austradia created governance complexity without solving the economics.
- **Assess parent company health as a dependency**: Topshop Australia's model was entirely dependent on UK Topshop — which itself was in structural decline.

### Case 9 — Esprit

**Overview**

Esprit, the German-headquartered but Hong Kong-listed fashion brand, had operated in Australia since the 1980s — making its exit in 2018 a three-decade relationship gone sour. Despite multiple turnaround attempts, ANZ operations were loss-making for years before the plug was pulled.

**Australia Entry Strategy**

Esprit entered Australia decades before the current fast-fashion era, establishing a strong department store concession model (particularly through Myer) and standalone retail stores. At the time of exit, it had 16 retail stores, 38 Myer concessions, and 13 factory outlets — 67 directly managed points of sale across ANZ.

**What Went Wrong in Australia**

- **Fast fashion disruption**: The arrival of H&M, Zara, Uniqlo, and others from the mid-2000s onwards commoditised the fashion segment Esprit occupied — mid-market, casual basics — at significantly lower price points.
- **Cost structure could not absorb the competition**: Despite "intensive efforts" to turn around the business, the cost of running 67 stores made profitability impossible once the competitive intensity increased.
- **Global brand dilution**: Esprit's global design vision had lost relevance — Australian consumers who once aspired to the brand found international competitors offered more trend-forward designs at lower prices.
- **Revenue scale too small to justify maintenance**: ANZ contributed HK$297 million (approximately US$38M) in FY2017 — less than 2% of global revenue. The economics of maintaining ANZ could never justify the management bandwidth.

**Outcome in Australia**

In May 2018, Esprit announced closure of all 67 ANZ stores by year end, with 350 staff losing jobs. The one-off exit costs were estimated at HK$150–200 million (approximately A$25–33M). The company redirected resources to China, Hong Kong, Taiwan, Singapore, and Malaysia.

**Lessons for MES**
- **ANZ market tenure does not insulate against structural shifts**: Being established for 30+ years provides no protection when the competitive landscape fundamentally changes.
- **Revenue contribution of <2% of global revenue signals strategic expendability**: If Australia is too small to matter on a global P&L, it will be cut in a downturn.
- **Department store concession models amplify vulnerability**: Esprit's reliance on Myer as a channel meant its fate was tied to Myer's declining traffic and footfall.
- **Fast-fashion disruption arrives slowly, then all at once**: The erosion of Esprit's position took years; the exit happened fast.
- **Exit costs must be budgeted before entry**: The A$25–33M exit bill was a material cost on a business generating A$50M in revenue.

### Case 10 — Gap

**Overview**

Gap's Australian franchise was operated by OrotonGroup — a struggling listed Australian luxury retailer — rather than by Gap itself. When OrotonGroup's financial performance deteriorated, the Gap franchise was the first thing to be cut, resulting in the closure of all six Australian stores by January 2018.

**Australia Entry Strategy**

Gap entered Australia through an exclusive franchise arrangement with OrotonGroup, which held the licence to operate Gap stores in the country. This gave Gap market access without direct capital investment — but transferred the execution risk to a franchise partner that was simultaneously managing its own struggling core brand.

**What Went Wrong in Australia**

- **Wrong partner selection**: OrotonGroup was undergoing a strategic crisis of its own — CEO churn, declining core sales, and weak trading conditions. Entrusting Gap's growth in Australia to a distressed partner was structurally risky.
- **Franchise model divorced execution from brand standards**: When OrotonGroup's financial performance deteriorated, there was no incentive or capital to invest in the Gap stores.
- **Soft Australian retail demand**: The Australian fashion market in 2015–18 was experiencing intense competitive pressure from H&M, Zara, and Uniqlo, which had entered on a fully owned basis with lower cost structures.
- **Limited scale**: Six stores was never sufficient to build brand awareness or achieve supply chain economies.

**Outcome in Australia**

In August 2017, OrotonGroup announced it would discontinue the Gap franchise. All six stores closed by end of January 2018. Gap had no direct Australian operation and simply ceased to have a physical presence.

**Lessons for MES**
- **Partner selection is a strategic decision, not a convenience**: Franchising to a distressed local partner transfers entry risk without transferring execution capability.
- **Small-scale pilots under a franchisee are rarely recoverable**: Six stores was insufficient to learn, adapt, and compete in Australian fashion retail.
- **The franchise model suits strong brands with strong partners**: Pairing a mid-strength brand (Gap's declining global position by 2017) with a financially distressed franchisee is a recipe for exit.
- **Direct ownership of critical international operations may be necessary**: In markets where brand standards are critical, a fully owned operation gives more control than a franchise.
- **Local competitive conditions matter more than global brand equity**: Gap's global name offered little protection against locally owned H&M and Zara with lower cost bases.

### Case 11 — Carl's Jr.

**Overview**

The US burger chain Carl's Jr. stormed into Australia in 2016 promising hundreds of stores and a bold challenge to McDonald's and KFC. By July 2024, its master franchisee CJ's Group had entered voluntary administration with 24 stores affected and 20 immediately closed.

**Australia Entry Strategy**

Carl's Jr. used a master franchisee model, with CJ's Group Pty Ltd independently owning and operating 24 restaurants and holding a master licence over another 25 sub-franchised locations. The chain expanded aggressively, targeting regional NSW, Queensland, and Victoria.

**What Went Wrong in Australia**

- **Over-expansion into regional/low-density locations**: The brand placed a significant number of stores in regional areas described as "the middle of nowhere" — locations with insufficient foot traffic to sustain QSR economics.
- **Reduced discretionary spending**: The cost-of-living pressures of 2023–24 hit fast-casual spending hard, particularly in regional and suburban markets.

- **Marketing never connected with locals**: Unlike McDonald's and KFC, which had decades of Australian brand equity, Carl's Jr. never established a distinctive local identity.

- **QSR expansion into regional markets requires verified population density and foot traffic data**: Not all Australian cities and regions support international QSR economics.

- **Brand differentiation is existential in crowded QSR markets**: Without a compelling reason to choose Carl's Jr. over McDonald's, KFC, or Hungry Jack's, Australians defaulted to the familiar.
- **Price sensitivity in cost-of-living downturns hits QSR hard**: Discretionary food spend is the first to compress — model downside scenarios explicitly.
- **Sub-franchise survival after master franchise collapse is instructive**: The 25 independently operated stores survived while the directly owned stores failed — sub-franchisees had stronger local skin in the game.

### Case 12 — WeWork

**Overview**

WeWork expanded aggressively into Australia during 2018–2020, signing long leases at peak market rates across Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane, and Perth. Its global bankruptcy in November 2023 sent shockwaves through the Australian commercial property market, with the company ultimately handing back keys to at least four Australian sites and renegotiating all 15 remaining leases.

**Australia Entry Strategy**

WeWork entered Australia by signing long-term leases (some up to 15 years) on premium CBD office space across four cities, subletting to startups, SMEs, and enterprise clients on flexible terms. At its peak in Australia, WeWork held approximately 100,000 sqm across 15 locations, with estimated future lease liabilities of A$900 million.

**What Went Wrong in Australia**

- **Long-term leases at peak market rates**: WeWork locked in expensive, decade-long lease commitments at a market peak, then tried to sublet at prices that couldn't justify the base cost.
- **The model was real estate arbitrage dressed as tech**: Once investors recognised WeWork was a leveraged property play — not a tech company — its US$47B valuation collapsed, killing the capital-raising machine that funded losses.
- **COVID destroyed demand**: The shift to remote work in 2020–21 emptied coworking spaces globally. WeWork's Australian occupancy never fully recovered.
- **Chapter 11 spillover**: While Australian subsidiaries were not formally included in the US Chapter 11 filing, landlords and tenants were deeply uncertain about the entity's stability. WeWork Australia posted a loss and carried A$930.4M in lease liabilities for the next five years.
- **Negotiated exits were necessary but costly**: WeWork had to renegotiate multiple Australian leases in late 2023–early 2024, closing sites in Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane, and Perth.

**Outcome in Australia**

WeWork completed lease renegotiations in July 2024, retaining 15 "highest quality and best-performing" locations across four cities. The outcome represents a dramatic downsizing — from a $900M liability footprint to a smaller, sustainable portfolio. WeWork emerged from global Chapter 11 in June 2024 and continues operating in Australia, though as a shadow of its original ambition.

- **Avoid long-term lease commitments in early-stage market entry**: Flexible, shorter-term lease structures reduce fixed cost exposure during market validation.
- **Proptech/SaaS company valuations need scrutiny**: WeWork's "tech premium" disguised a highly leveraged property business. Understand what you're actually paying for when acquiring or partnering.
- **Macro shocks (COVID, rate rises) can destroy coworking demand overnight**: Model catastrophic scenarios for demand-dependent businesses.
- **Australian landlords are sophisticated counterparties**: Long commercial lease negotiations in Australia carry material obligations — legal advice before signing is mandatory.
- **Parent company health directly impacts subsidiary stability**: WeWork Australia had no independent capital structure — entirely at the mercy of its US parent.

### Case 13 — Amazon Australia (Delayed / Underwhelming Entry)

**Overview**

Amazon launched in Australia in December 2017 to enormous anticipation — and immediate disappointment. The world's largest e-commerce company was roundly criticised for a product range described as "sparse," pricing that wasn't competitive, and a decision to block Australians from its global site in response to GST legislation — creating months of consumer backlash.

**Australia Entry Strategy**

Amazon launched Amazon.com.au with a single fulfilment centre near Melbourne, 60 million products (compared to 500 million on its US site), and initially no Amazon Prime offering. The launch was described by analysts at Citi and Morgan Stanley as underwhelming — discounts were fewer than expected and several key brand categories were missing.

**What Went Wrong in Australia**

- **Undersized launch**: A continent the size of the contiguous US with a single warehouse meant delivery times and costs were not competitive with established local retailers offering click-and-collect and next-day delivery.
- **GST blocking controversy**: In July 2018, Amazon blocked Australian customers from accessing its international sites in response to new GST legislation requiring collection on sub-$1,000 imports. The move caused significant consumer backlash, and Amazon reversed the decision in November 2018 after intense pressure.
- **Limited product range**: Australian consumers were redirected from Amazon.com (500M+ products) to a sparse local site — a severe regression in experience that alienated early adopters.
- **Price expectations**: The media had built up Amazon as a price-disrupting force. When prices were similar to, or higher than, local retailers in many categories, the perceived value proposition evaporated.
- **Long game execution**: It took Amazon approximately 5–7 years to build meaningful market share — a timeline that suggests the launch was underinvested relative to the expectations it created.

**Outcome in Australia**

Amazon Australia is now growing — reaching 10% of online shopping spend by 2024 and 60% consumer penetration by 2026. However, the underwhelming launch created years of disadvantage against Kmart, Catch (before its collapse), and eBay, which used the time to solidify their positions. Amazon is now dominant in the pure-play online marketplace segment but never triggered the "retail apocalypse" predicted at launch.

**Lessons for MES**
- **Launch infrastructure must match launch expectations**: An underwhelming launch is worse than a delayed one — it shapes perception for years.
- **Regulatory compliance is a market entry pre-condition**: Australia's GST legislation was foreseeable — Amazon's decision to block users rather than comply was a strategic error.
- **E-commerce in Australia faces structural logistics challenges**: Vast geography and low population density mean single-warehouse models cannot replicate US delivery speeds.
- **Consumer expectations must be managed proactively**: Years of media hype about Amazon's arrival created expectation gaps that the launch couldn't fill.
- **Patience and capital depth matter in e-commerce market entry**: The 5–7-year timeline to dominance in new markets is a real benchmark for planning.

### Case 14 — Catch.com.au (Wesfarmers Acquisition)

**Overview**

Catch.com.au was Australia's original daily deals and online marketplace pioneer. Acquired by Wesfarmers in 2019 for A$230 million, the platform accumulated nearly A$450 million in trading losses under Wesfarmers' ownership before being wound down in January 2025.

**Australia Entry Strategy**

This case is instructive not as a foreign company entering Australia, but as an example of a strategic acquirer failing to integrate or protect a digital native from incoming international competition. Wesfarmers bought Catch to build e-commerce capabilities, but left it operating largely independently.

**What Went Wrong in Australia**

- **Amazon's scale overwhelmed Catch's position**: When Wesfarmers bought Catch in 2019, Amazon had been in Australia for less than two years. By 2025, Amazon had 10% of online shopping spend, Temu had 20% — together dismantling Catch's competitive position.
- **Marketplace economics require massive scale**: As Wesfarmers CEO Rob Scott acknowledged, "standalone, broad-based marketplaces require significant scale and traffic to achieve profitability. International players are better able to leverage their global scale."
- **Synergies never materialised**: Despite the stated goal of building e-commerce expertise across Kmart and Target, Catch was never integrated into Wesfarmers' ecosystem in a way that created competitive advantage.
- **Product range differentiation lost**: Under Wesfarmers, Catch lost the sharp buying and own-product capabilities that had made its original founders (Gabby and Hezi Leibovich) successful.
- **Fulfilment centres operating at <50% capacity**: A signal of structural over-investment in fixed costs relative to achievable volume.

**Outcome in Australia**

Wesfarmers announced Catch's wind-down in January 2025, with final closure by June 2025 (Q4 FY2025). Approximately 200 jobs were lost. The fulfilment centres were transferred to Kmart Group, which operates them more cost-effectively. Cumulative losses under Wesfarmers approached A$450M.

**Lessons for MES**
- **Acquisition of a local digital player buys time, not immunity**: Foreign competition (Amazon, Temu) will eventually outscale any locally owned marketplace without continuous capital investment.
- **Marketplace integration must be deep and immediate**: Half-measures that leave an acquired business "operating independently" without the full benefit of group resources create the worst of both worlds.
- **Digital platforms have scale thresholds, not gradual curves**: Below a certain transaction volume, a marketplace becomes structurally unprofitable regardless of management quality.
- **Assess acquisition rationale carefully**: Wesfarmers admitted it bought Catch for capability, not for Catch itself — a rationale that rarely justifies the acquisition price.
- **Competitive due diligence must include forthcoming entrants, not just current players**: Amazon was "new" in 2019; Temu didn't exist. Strategic planning must scenario-model for new entrants.

### Case 15 — Groupon

**Overview**

Groupon's entry into Australia was one of the messiest of any tech company — it arrived to find its own brand name, domain (groupon.com.au), and trademark already registered by a local competitor, Scoopon, forcing the American coupon giant to launch under the brand "Stardeals."

**Australia Entry Strategy**

Groupon entered Australia around 2010–11, intending to replicate its global group-buying model. The core strategy was daily deal emails offering heavily discounted products and services from local merchants.

**What Went Wrong in Australia**

- **Trademark and domain squatting**: Australian competitor Scoopon (owned by CatchOfTheDay founders Gabby and Hezi Leibovich) had pre-registered "Groupon Pty Limited" as a company name, registered the Groupon.com.au domain, and filed for the Groupon trademark in Australia — just seven days before Groupon's own filing. Groupon was forced to launch as "Stardeals" while fighting multiple legal battles simultaneously.
- **Late to a crowded market**: By the time Groupon entered Australia, Scoopon, LivingSocial, and local variants were already established. Groupon's offer letter to buy Scoopon's assets was initially accepted (at A$286,000) and then rejected — a classic IP exploitation play.
- **Merchant model didn't suit the market**: Groupon's model (heavily discounted deals that attracted deal-seekers who rarely became loyal customers) drew sustained criticism from Australian merchants, many of whom found the economics destructive.
- **Fading global model**: Groupon's business model began showing structural cracks globally around 2012–13, undermining its value proposition before it had established itself locally.

**Outcome in Australia**

Groupon eventually obtained its brand name in Australia after litigation, but never achieved the dominant market position it held in the US or Europe. The Australian group-buying market ultimately consolidated and declined, with Groupon's Australian operation becoming a marginal, low-investment presence. The platform continues to operate in Australia but with far less relevance than intended.

**Lessons for MES**
- **Trademark and domain registration is a Day-1 entry task**: Register your brand name, domain, and trademark in Australia before announcing your entry publicly.
- **IP protection failures cause multi-year competitive disadvantages**: Years spent as "Stardeals" while fighting legal battles is market share lost.
- **Understand local competitor preparedness**: Scoopon's founders were sophisticated operators who anticipated Groupon's entry and pre-empted it deliberately.
- **Late entry to a crowded two-sided marketplace is structurally disadvantaged**: The merchant side and consumer side were already matched by local incumbents.
- **Business model validation in the local market must precede scale**: Groupon never resolved whether its merchant model worked in Australia before investing in the brand dispute.

### Case 19 — Volt Bank

**Overview**

Volt Bank was Australia's first purely digital bank to receive a banking licence from APRA (2019), surfing the government's post-Royal Commission push for neobank competition. Three years later, it surrendered its licence and returned deposits — becoming the first Australian neobank to fail.

**Australia Entry Strategy**

Volt entered with the backing of APRA's new restricted banking licence framework introduced in 2018 following the Royal Commission into banking misconduct. It launched a consumer savings product in 2019 with over 40,000 early-access users and planned to expand into loans and mortgages — the key revenue driver.

**What Went Wrong in Australia**

- **Pandemic timing destroyed the expansion plan**: COVID-19 hit in March 2020 — just as Volt was preparing to launch mortgages, its path to profitability. The Australian government imposed rate suppression policies and uncertainty that made the business case for neobank mortgage origination untenable.
- **Funding could not be secured**: Volt raised capital in multiple rounds but was unable to close the final funding needed to sustain operations while awaiting profitability.
- **B2C banking economics are brutal**: Volt offered a high-interest savings account (funded by the spread), but with near-zero official interest rates, the spread disappeared.
- **Scale was insufficient**: By April 2022, Volt had only A$113 million in deposits and A$80 million in home loans — a fraction of the scale needed for viability in Australian retail banking.

- **Regulatory encouragement is not the same as a viable business model**: APRA actively encouraged neobank formation — but the market dynamics (rates, scale requirements, incumbent dominance) were unchanged.
- **Time-to-profitability must be stress-tested against catastrophic scenarios**: Volt's business case assumed conditions that changed within 12 months of launch.
- **B2C banking requires substantial scale to absorb regulatory compliance costs**: Australia's banking regulatory environment is among the most demanding globally.
- **Fintech founders should assess the "profitability cliff"**: The point at which a fintech must transition from subsidised growth to sustainable revenue often arrives before funding can bridge the gap.

### Case 20 — Debenhams

**Overview**

British department store Debenhams — a 240+ year-old retail institution — entered Australia with bold ambitions for 10 stores. It opened exactly one, on Collins Street in Melbourne, and quietly shut it in the late 2010s without ever expanding beyond that single footprint.

**Australia Entry Strategy**

Debenhams planned a 10-store rollout across Australia, entering a market where Myer and David Jones — two entrenched, Australian-native department stores with strong brand loyalty, prime locations, and extensive supplier relationships — dominated the department store sector.

**What Went Wrong in Australia**

- **Department store market was already served by entrenched incumbents**: Myer and David Jones had decades of brand equity, loyalty programs, and access to premium store sites. Debenhams offered nothing differentiated enough to displace them.
- **One store was never a viable market entry**: A single Collins Street store could not build the supply chain efficiency, brand awareness, or customer data that department store economics require.
- **No clear differentiation**: The Debenhams model — fashion, beauty, homeware — was indistinguishable from what Myer and David Jones already offered.
- **UK brand not well-known in Australia**: Unlike in the UK, where Debenhams had 240 years of heritage, Australian consumers had no existing loyalty or emotional connection to the brand.
- **Timing**: The late 2000s–early 2010s entry coincided with the beginning of the Australian department store sector's decline, driven by online retail and international fast fashion.

The single Melbourne store closed in the late 2010s. Debenhams today has an online presence in Australia but no physical retail operation. It serves as an example of an underfunded, under-researched market entry into a structurally challenged sector.

**Lessons for MES**
- **A single-store pilot in a capital city is not a market test**: Department store economics require multiple locations to validate supply chain efficiency and marketing return.
- **Assess structural sector health before entering**: The Australian department store sector was entering a cyclical and structural decline at the time Debenhams arrived.
- **Brand heritage does not translate across geographies**: UK brand equity means nothing to Australian consumers who have never experienced the brand.
- **Differentiation is mandatory in mature retail sectors**: "We also sell fashion, beauty, and homeware" is not a market entry thesis when Myer and David Jones already do.
- **Underfunded pilots produce underfunded results**: A commitment to 10 stores that becomes one is not a pilot — it is a half-decision that produces a half-outcome.

## Cross-Cutting Themes for MES Analysis

### Why Australia is Harder Than It Looks

Australia presents a deceptive trap for international market entrants. The combination of English language, political stability, strong GDP per capita, and cultural familiarity with global brands creates the impression that entry will be straightforward. The 20 cases above reveal a consistent pattern of failure driven by the following structural factors:

1. **Geography and logistics**: A continent the size of the US with the population of Texas. Supply chain and distribution economics are brutally unforgiving for companies used to US or European density.
2. **Entrenched local incumbents**: In almost every sector — coffee, hardware, supermarkets, department stores, food delivery, BNPL — Australian markets feature dominant incumbents with deep supplier relationships, prime real estate, and loyal customers.
3. **Cultural specificity**: Australians have well-developed preferences in food, lifestyle, sport, and retail that differ substantially from their US or UK counterparts. Assumptions about cultural overlap are consistently wrong.
4. **Market size constraints**: 26 million people means many business models that work at US-scale are structurally unviable at Australian scale — especially SaaS, BNPL, and marketplace businesses that require minimum transaction volumes to be profitable.
5. **Regulatory environment**: Australia's consumer protection laws (ACL), financial services regulation (APRA, ASIC), gig economy regulations, and tax framework (GST) are sophisticated and non-negotiable. Companies that treat Australia as a "copy-paste" of US regulation face serious legal exposure.
6. **Franchise and partner risk**: Multiple cases (Gap, Topshop, Carl's Jr., Masters/Lowe's) illustrate that using a local partner or franchisee to reduce upfront investment often transfers execution risk to a party with misaligned incentives, inadequate capital, or its own operational problems.
